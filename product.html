Mobile design, product design

Smart Soles is a smart wearable insole that encourages seniors to take charge of their health.

This is the companion app that enables seniors to track their balance over time and improve balance + health through custom exercises with feedback.

UX/UI Design, User Interviews, Competitive Analysis, Technical Research, Product Validation

Team

Alana la Rue, Christopher Tong, Karen Hong, Ryan Vo, Pranav Arya and myself

Role

UX/UI Design Lead, Research, Product Strategy

Duration

Sept 2019 - April 2020

Problem scope.

Smart Soles was born out of a tech venture capstone project, after discovering that falls are a leading cause of injuries and deaths in seniors. In the states, 1 senior dies every 19 minutes as a result of a fall.

My team and I were interested in solving the issue of senior falls and how to reduce falls in a way that would be readily adopted by senior users, scalable in production and would help seniors maintain their quality of life into old age.

Overview.

Smart Soles is a smart wearable in the form of an insole, where it collects balance data through force-sensing resistors. This data is then wirelessly sent to be interpreted by our machine learning algorithms, and the data is then relayed to the users in our companion app. For this case study, I will focus on the UX/UI design in parallel to the physical product development for the entire product cycle.

Not all the designs from this case study will be launched. Due to COVID-19, we have stopped our development up to version 2, however, the final designs are a representation of the Smart Soles app if it were continued for market launch.

Product research

Research methods:
1) literature review, 2) industry review, 3) whitespace diagram, 4) petal diagram, 5) requirements & constraints

A deep-dive of literature and industry review revealed that senior falls are a growing problem as the world's aging population continues to increase. Moreover, there are little products on the market with a preventative approach to senior healthcare, as most products are reactive in nature eg. Life Alert (reactive as in seeking help or treatment once a fall has occured). While we understand that the deterioration of balance is often due to multiple factors eg. sensory loss, we are focusing on improving motor connections and consumer accessibility to personal health.

Consistent exercise and balance training has been shown to reduce falls by 36%. Market research suggests that the wearable industry has been steadily increasing the past years, and is projected to do so in the future as consumers shift to take control over their own health. We derived that the best form factor for a senior wearable could be an insole - as it is unintrusive, could used in most occasions, and is something that you could just set and forget.

Technical and academic research provided support that we could create a feasible MVP with force-sensing resistors, machine learning, and a senior-oriented app. The whitespace analysis of competing smart insoles demonstrates how our technical features differentiate, while the petal diagram indicates how our product will provide value in the current market environment.

User research.

Research methods: 1) semi-structured user interviews + stakeholder interviews, 2) surveys, 3) competitive analysis, 4) observations, 5) persona

After determining our product goals through research, it was time to gather user data to inform our product design decisions. We conducted over 100 user interviews, including seniors, doctors, physiotherapists, adult children (30s-50s year old) and grandchildren (>30 year old). I went to senior centres, asked seniors buying groceries on the streets, reached out to healthcare professionals and chatted with adults who had aging parents.

This allowed us to understand more holistically of the existing experiences, feelings and frustrations from all those involved in this ecosystem. It also allowed us to validate our product scope and user segments.

From the research conducted, an average user might be someone like Annie. Her main goal is to maintain her quality of life in a non-invasive, personalized and preventative way. I also analyzed popular wearable products, such as FitBit, Apple Health, Garmin, Up by Jawbone (discontinued), Under Armour Digitally Connected shoes, and other wearable tracking apps to gather user insights.

I decided to place an emphasis on the Fitbit app, the Apple Health app and Google Fit for competitive analysis, since they hold a strong presence in the wearable industry. I also observed how seniors tended to use their smart phones and tablets to gain an understanding of their user behaviours on mobile devices. This, in combination with online research and using different types of fitness tracking apps, gave me some leads into key insights.

Initial insights.

Methods: 1) thematic analysis

The research so far validated our product scope and user problems. However, with all the information collected, it was difficult to identify and prioritize pain points and necessary features. I decided to review the data collected and group them via thematic analysis.

But that only raised more questions... I decided that it was better to fail first, then reiterate as I go. For the first design, I focused on meeting technical functions and what I assumed users may want.

Prototype v1

With what I had so far, how can we create an app experience that had kept senior users in mind and fulfilled our technical features? Some initial designs:

There came an opportunity to quickly build and break our product at nwHacks 2020, where we were able to successfully create our first prototype (hardware and software) in under 24 hours.

The app got the job done and it was built using ReactNative. It also somewhat matches the initial design (close enough haha), but from this experience, I realized that the design was too vague and non-descriptive. For seniors, they often need breadcrumb cues to remind them of the navigation and intended use due to a reduced cognitive capacity. It also made me think more critically about the visual and UI design, and how I can design in a way that allowed customization and consistency.

Reiterate + reframe

Methods: 1) 2x2 matrix, 2) reframing the question, 3) user flow

I decided to go back to senior homes, as well talk to more adult children and grandparents to get user feedback. This time, I asked more specific questions focused on the app design, current screens and user behaviour.

From the previous research and initial prototype, I prioritized the feedback and technical features based on its importance to users and its importance to our physical product. These assumptions are based on conversations and suggestions made with seniors, healthcare providers, adult childre/grandchildren, as well as my team. Tracking balance, showing progress and the exercise follow-along features were fundamental to the core product. In addition, I wanted to explore the gamification/incentives aspect as part of the app design.
